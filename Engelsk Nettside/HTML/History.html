Timeline of Califonian History

1850 California Becomes the 31st State

On September 9, 1850, California formally joined the USA as the 31st State. This rapid route to statehood became driven by way of the influx of people at some point in the Gold Rush, which greatly boosted California’s population and financial importance.

1869 Completion of the Transcontinental Railroad

The completion of the Transcontinental Railroad on May 10, 1869, connected California to the eastern United States revolutionizing trade and travel. This milestone spurred population growth, connected California’s agricultural financial system to the rest of the united states of america, and converted cities like Sacramento and San Francisco into key hubs.

1906 San Francisco Earthquake

On April 18, 1906, San Francisco was devastated by one of the most powerful earthquakes in U.S. History, estimated to be a magnetude of 7.9. The quake and following fires destroyed most of the city, killing hundreds and leaving nearly 300,000 people homeless.

1911 Women’s Suffrage in California

California became the lead in women’s rights on October 10 1911, when they granted women the right to vote nearly a decade before the rest of the U.S.

1923 The Hollywood Sign Built

IIn 1923, the iconic Hollywood Sign was originally constructed as an advertisement for a real estate development called "Hollywoodland." Over time, the sign became a symbol of the burgeoning film industry and the cultural rise of Los Angeles as the entertainment capital of the world.

1937 Opening of the Golden Gate Bridge

The Golden Gate Bridge Opens On May 27, 1937, the Golden Gate Bridge was completed, marking a major engineering achievement. Spanning the Golden Gate Strait, the bridge connected San Francisco to Marin County and rapidly became one of the most renowned landmarks in the world.

1942 Japanese-American Internment During WWII

Following the attack on Pearl Harbor, President Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066 on February 19, 1942, leading to the internment of more than 120,000 Japanese Americans, many from California. This dark chapter in U.S. history led to the creation of internment camps such as Manzanar, located in California's Owens Valley.

1965 – The Watts Riots

From August 11 to 16, 1965, the Watts Riots erupted in Los Angeles, a significant moment in the Civil Rights Movement. The six days of unrest were triggered by racial tensions and police brutality, highlighting deep-seated inequalities in Los Angeles and across the country.

1967 – The Summer of Love

In 1967, San Francisco’s Haight-Ashbury district became the epicenter of the Summer of Love, a cultural and social movement focused on peace, love, and countercultural values. The event solidified California’s role in the counterculture and civil rights movements of the 1960s.

1984 Los Angeles Hosts the Summer Olympics

The 1984 Summer Olympics in Los Angeles marked a highly successful event, helping revitalize the city. The games were notable for their financial success, the introduction of corporate sponsorship, and showcasing LA as a vibrant and modern global city.

1994 Northridge Earthquake

On January 17, 1994, a powerful earthquake struck the Northridge area of Los Angeles, causing widespread damage and killing 57 people. With a magnitude of 6.7, it was one of the costliest natural disasters in U.S. history, but the rapid recovery displayed California’s resilience.

2003 Recall of Governor Gray Davis

On October 7, 2003, California Governor Gray Davis was recalled, leading to one of the most high-profile political shifts in state history. Actor Arnold Schwarzenegger was elected as governor in his place, marking a rare and dramatic political event.

2020 California’s Role in Battling COVID-19

The year 2020 marked a challenging time as California became a key battleground in the fight against the COVID-19 pandemic. The state issued the first statewide stay-at-home order, and its response, including vaccine distribution and pandemic relief efforts, played a crucial role in managing the crisis on a national scale.

2021 California Becomes the First State to Ban Gas-Powered Cars by 2035

In 2021, California made history by becoming the first state to pass a law banning the sale of new gas-powered vehicles by 2035, aiming to lead the nation in combating climate change and pushing for a transition to electric vehicles.
